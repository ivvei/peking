What is Peking Opera?

Purpose of Website

Peking Opera, also referred to as Beijing Opera or 京劇 (Jīngjù), is the most prominent form of Chinese Opera. It is an art involving acting, stories, music, acrobatics, as well as martial arts. It sprung from Beijing in the north east of China. Arising in the Qing court, it became fully developed and known by the mid-19th century.

The performances, performed across China today, depict Chinese cultural traditions revolving around history in Beijing, Tianjin and Shanghai.

History to Chinese Culture

Peking Opera: Insight

Chinese festivities in Beijing such as Chinese New Year, the Dragon Boat Festival, the Mid-Autumn Festival, Golden Week, Dongzhi Festival, mark dates for when the most popular stories from famous Tang dynasty works such as The Drunken Concubine, to The Orphan of Zhao revolving around themes of revenge, are performed to larger audiences. Additionally, on these days, companies create stories dedicated to the festival.

A festival in Peking Opera is Little New Year’s Day on February 4, where popular companies such as the China National Peking Opera Company perform their last performance before actors seal the boxes of performance equipment to perform a final masterpiece of each play.

Musical Analysis

The Drunken Concubine

The Drunken Concubine, or A Drunken Beauty, is a classic play considered a masterpiece, sung by the Concubine herself. It is sung using Beijing dialect, with librettos composed to a strict set of rules prioritising rhymes as well as forms. As it is a play, it tells stories of history, politics, and society, through four main types of characters: sheng, dan, jing, and chou. Here, it is sung by a character depicting Dan (woman).

Motifs & Characteristics

Pitch of Voice

The ugly, exaggerated costumes in Peking Opera are meant to be symbolic rather than realistic, they follow other traditional Chinese arts, prioritising meaning. Literal aspects are removed or stylised to represent intangible emotions. An example of this is removing sharp lines, as a common stylisation method is roundness. The costumes are used as symbolic expressions to convey traditional Chinese core values, packed with symbols. Dragons and phoenix are the most prominent (representing strength, health, and fortune), with birds, tigers, wild geese expressing Chinese core values, which came from imperial ideology based on Confucian ideas, practiced throughout the Qing Dynasty, where Peking Opera was developed.
